Cloudiness

This graph shows that cities in this dataset are mostly concentrated in the 0% cloud zone or the 70-90% cloud zone in the month of January. Furthermore, most cloudy cities in the Northern Hemisphere are concentrated above 40 degrees latitude, while most cities with the highest values of cloudiness in the Southern Hemisphere are between 0 and 40 degrees latitude. In the month of January, the cities with the greatest cloudiness are located up to 20 degrees of latitude in the southern hemisphere, where it is summer at this time of year. This is explained by warm, humid air cooling as it rises, therefore retaining less moisture, and water vapor being condensed into cloud particles. According to NASA's Earth Observatory, "there are three broad bands where Earth's skies are likely to be cloudy: a narrow band near the equator and two wider bands at mid-latitudes." (...) "While clouds tend to form where air rises as part of atmospheric circulation patterns, descending air inhibits cloud formation. As air descends between about 15 and 30 degrees north and south from the equator, clouds are rare and deserts are common at this latitude." The graph also confirms these observations by showing a higher concentration of cities with 0% cloudiness close to 15 and 30 degrees of latitude, mainly in the Northern Hemisphere.

Reference: NASA's Earth Observatory
